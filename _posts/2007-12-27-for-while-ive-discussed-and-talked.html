--- layout: post title: Music and the mp3 bomb date: '2007-12-27T23:37:00.000-02:00' author: Alejandro tags: - mp3 - music - media - future - digital modified_time: '2008-12-09T22:24:56.040-02:00' thumbnail: http://3.bp.blogspot.com/_12j-6WCLPuM/R3RelFr0IbI/AAAAAAAAADg/4k96OpEEGpE/s72-c/radio.jpg blogger_id: tag:blogger.com,1999:blog-3848561.post-66317655078446222 blogger_orig_url: http://alepanizza.blogspot.com/2007/12/for-while-ive-discussed-and-talked.html --- For a while I've discussed and talked about digital distribution of media, but at the end of 2007, the doom of physical formats, seems inevitable and music will be first.
Apple and Amazon are selling DRM-free music for several month now, no word on sales , but I am willing to bet that the format switch increased sales.

Amazon has announced a deal with Universal to sell their entire music catalog DRM free , and that's a lot of songs....

In other recent developments, several bands have taken it one step further :


Radiohead, have released ( or pre-released) their music for free online before the physical CD.


Nine Inch Nails has the digital edition of his latest abum online, and uses youtube to post music videos, besides encouraging fans to remix and upload their songs many bands are following. Even new bands in Uruguay are releasing singles online like "Closet" see here.

And the thing is that for big groups the money for album sales is not the biggest income: between production, distribution, publicity , etc.
They can however make money by touring, and that can't be copied...

For unknown bands, the same is true but, for different reasons, when I can get any music, the best publicity is the song being distributed worldwide for free, that can lead to a record deal or a concert offer.

In essence my question is "music" as in "the music business" still an scalable profession, or has gone back to the non scalable state it was before recording was invented?
