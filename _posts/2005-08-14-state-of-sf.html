I've been reading science fiction since I was 13 ( that would be 1983), My first SF novels were really crappy paperback shit. Then I met the "gods" of SF : Asimov (Foundation Series, the Robots novels), A.C.Clarke, Heinlein, Phil K. Dick, Stevenson, J.Blish, Brian Aldiss, Paul Anderson, etc (too many to name them all)
It was old material, but new to me.

The next step, was to look for something new, that part was difficult, having read excelent SF, the novels at that time seemed not up to the same standard. But I continued to read anyway, more SF than anything else, until I came across William Gibson, needless to say it was something new and clever, since I was already into Computers, naturally I was hooked into this new style. I liked most Gibson novels, but specially the short stories and Idoru.

Then, there was a huge wave of Gibson, imitators, I read some, nothing interesting...

During the 90's I went back to classic ( space opera, big scenarios, evil aliens) SF, primary I've been reading Jack Vance, and mostly Iain Banks "culture" series, if you haven't read it I do recommend to try at least "The Player of Games", "The use of Weapons" and "Consider Phlebas" these are his best novels.

Now that the initial Gibson future is almost here :-), fans like me have a lot of free material online , and is not bad at all.

Aside from the usual "hard" science fiction ( of wich I consider Will Mccarty, and his "Queendom of Sol" series, the best current writer) there are two new styles that I find very interesting, the first one is what I call post-gibson, wich is not a very adequate name, buy I cannot think of a better one, ( that woud be "wired SF"), this novels explore the social consequences of hyperconnected societies, and global markets. Some of them like Cory Doctorow, and recent Hugo winner Charles Stross are pretty good and even offer their work under CC licence, so you can read them for free.
The second style is what you can call "historical" SF ( it's more like Fantasy Fiction), where the authors explore historical events ( even pre-historical ones) and intermix them with fictional characters and/or alternate realities.
In this line my favorites are Neal Stepenson's Cryptonomicon and The Barocque Cycle
and more recently Robert J. Sawyer who is a Canadian author, using Paleonthological extrapolations and parallel universes as a backdrop for wonderfull stories like Hominids , Humans and Hybrids.

So if anybody had read any of this authors/books and like to leave a comment, I would appreciate, and if you have an author to recommend that it's not mentioned in the post , please do so.
